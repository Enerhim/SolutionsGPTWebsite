1. Introduction

In real-life, pure substances are rare; almost all materials are mixtures, with solutions being one of the most important. This chapter covers the formation and properties of solutions—from dissolution and dynamic equilibrium to vapor pressure lowering, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, and azeotropes.

2. Key Definitions & Terms

  • Solution: A homogeneous mixture in which the solute is uniformly distributed within the solvent.
  • Solvent: The component present in the greatest amount that determines the physical state.
  • Solute: The substance dissolved in the solvent.
  • Dissolution: The process by which a solute dissolves in a solvent to form a homogeneous mixture.
  • Crystallization: The formation of solid crystals from a solution.
  • Dynamic Equilibrium: A state where the rates of dissolution and crystallization are equal in a saturated solution.
  • Saturated Solution: A solution that cannot dissolve any more solute at a given temperature and pressure.
  • Unsaturated Solution: A solution that can dissolve additional solute.
  • Supersaturated Solution: A metastable solution containing more solute than its equilibrium solubility.
  • Ideal Solution: A solution that obeys Raoult's law perfectly.
  • Non-Ideal Solution: A solution that deviates from Raoult’s law due to differing intermolecular forces.
  • Positive Deviation: When vapor pressure is higher than expected, typically due to weaker solute-solvent interactions.
  • Negative Deviation: When vapor pressure is lower than expected because of stronger solute-solvent interactions.
  • Azeotrope: A mixture that boils at a constant temperature because the vapor has the same composition as the liquid.
  • Colligative Properties: Properties that depend on the number of solute particles rather than their identity.
  • Ebullioscopic Constant (K₍b₎): Constant relating boiling point elevation to molality.
  • Cryoscopic Constant (K₍f₎): Constant relating freezing point depression to molality.
  • Osmosis: The movement of solvent through a semipermeable membrane from lower to higher solute concentration.
  • Reverse Osmosis: The process where pressure greater than the osmotic pressure forces solvent in the reverse direction.

3. Concentration, Dissolution & Dynamic Equilibrium

Concentration quantifies the amount of solute in a solution. Methods include mass percentage, volume percentage, mass by volume percentage, parts per million, mole fraction, molarity, and molality.

Mass % = (mass of solute / total mass of solution) × 100
Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution (L)
Molality (m) = moles of solute / mass of solvent (kg)

Dissolution is the process by which solute particles interact with solvent molecules to form a homogeneous mixture. In a saturated solution, dynamic equilibrium is reached when dissolution and crystallization occur at the same rate.

4. Key Laws and Equilibrium Concepts

Henry's Law

Henry’s Law applies to gases dissolved in liquids and states that at constant temperature, the concentration of a dissolved gas is directly proportional to its partial pressure.

C = kH × P

This law explains why gas solubility increases under high pressure and decreases with higher temperature.

Raoult's Law and Vapor Pressure Lowering

Raoult’s Law states that in an ideal solution, the vapor pressure of each volatile component is directly proportional to its mole fraction:

Psolvent = xsolvent × P°solvent

For a binary solution, the total vapor pressure is the sum of the partial pressures.

Azeotropes

An azeotrope is a mixture of liquids that boils at a constant temperature because the vapor has the same composition as the liquid. Depending on the interactions, azeotropes may have a minimum or maximum boiling point.

5. Colligative Properties

Colligative properties depend only on the number of solute particles in a solution, regardless of their identity. They include:

Relative Lowering of Vapor Pressure

ΔP / P° = xsolute

Solute particles reduce the number of solvent molecules at the surface, thereby lowering vapor pressure.

Boiling Point Elevation

ΔTb = Kb × m

Adding a non-volatile solute raises the boiling point of the solution.

Freezing Point Depression

ΔTf = Kf × m

Likewise, the freezing point is lowered by the presence of a solute.

Osmotic Pressure

π = M R T

Osmotic pressure is the pressure required to stop the net flow of solvent through a semipermeable membrane.

6. The van’t Hoff Factor (i)

The van’t Hoff factor (i) adjusts colligative property calculations for the effective number of particles in solution. It accounts for the dissociation of electrolytes or the association of solute molecules.

For Electrolytes: For instance, NaCl ideally dissociates into Na⁺ and Cl⁻ (i ≈ 2), though incomplete dissociation can lower i.

For Non-Electrolytes: Typically, i = 1 since they do not dissociate.

For Associating Solutes: Some molecules (e.g., acetic acid in benzene) dimerize, resulting in i < 1.

Boiling Point Elevation: ΔTb = iKbm
Freezing Point Depression: ΔTf = iKfm
Osmotic Pressure: π = iMRT

By comparing measured colligative properties with theoretical predictions, the extent of dissociation or association can be determined.

  • Concentration & Dissolution: Methods to express concentration and the process of dissolution leading to equilibrium.
  • Laws & Equilibria: Henry’s Law, Raoult’s Law, and Dalton’s Law that explain vapor pressure changes and composition effects.
  • Colligative Properties: How the number of solute particles affects vapor pressure, boiling point, freezing point, and osmotic pressure.
  • van’t Hoff Factor: Correction for dissociation/association effects in solutions.
